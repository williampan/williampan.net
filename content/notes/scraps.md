Many recent typefaces deviate from the traditional standard but are still well designed, ignoring any government standards that would radically mar the aesthetic form. I still prefer to avoid these when possible, as I believe the principles that guide the traditional standard are important. 

Japan officially simplified a few hundred kanji with the publication of the [*tōyō kanji*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C5%8Dy%C5%8D_kanji) 當用漢字 list in 1946, and the People's Republic of China simplified several thousand characters beginning with the publication of the [Chinese Character Simplification Scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Character_Simplification_Scheme) (漢字簡化方案 *Hanzi jianhua fang'an*) of 1956. Taiwan prescribed a number of vulgar variants with the publication of the [*Standard Form of National Characters*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Form_of_National_Characters) (國字標準字體 *Guozi biaozhun ziti*) in 1982, as did Hong Kong in the *[List of Graphemes of Commonly-Used Chinese Characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Graphemes_of_Commonly-Used_Chinese_Characters)* (常用字字形表 *Changyongzi zixing biao*) of 1986. 

Before the 20th century, there was one official character standard that was used across East Asia. This standard had changed very little since the widespread use of regular script in the Northern and Southern Dynasties, undergoing successive refinements over the centuries. During the script reforms of the 20th century, however, each jurisdiction that continued using the Chinese script prescribed its own standard, and now the situation is highly fragmented. Usually, typefaces that include Chinese characters are designed according to one of the following standards:

* the traditional standard
* the official standard of Taiwan
* the official standard of Hong Kong
* the official standard of Mainland China
* the official standard of Japan
* the official standard of Korea
