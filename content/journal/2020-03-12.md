We awoke to discover that Trump had banned all travel from Europe. I had begun to realize the gravity of the situation as well, as I read statistical analyses online. If I had planned this trip for a week later I wouldn't have come. The air when we stepped outside felt different. We began to refer to the virus as "the plague", imagining it as a pestilential green gas that filled the streets. By the Westgate shopping mall there was a man who played a clarinet, which in our delirium we called the plague flute. 

In the afternoon, Daniel, Henry, and I visited St. Catherine's College, a newer college, entirely Brutalist. We saw its dining hall, magnificently furnished; witnessed the faces in the bush; hung among the steel beams that angled down from a building's eaves. A stream lined the campus's southern edge, confined by concrete banks, lush and overgrown. 

On the way back, we stopped at the cemetery and marveled at the tombstones old and new. Gold leaf still glistened on some. The sun emerged briefly from the clouds when we were there, casting streaks of light across the stones. When we were leaving, we discovered one of the caskets was open, the bones inside already decomposed. 

After dark, Henry and I tried to attend a communion held at Keble College, but it was canceled: likely a plague measure. The architecture of Keble breaks from Oxford's usual Gothic: exteriors of layered red and white brick, animals and plants inlaid in every surface of the chapel interior. We saw the chapel, then wandered our way to the new addition: an incredible modernist dormitory, magnificent in scale. The interiors of the rooms, visible from the ground through floor-to-ceiling windows, have warm white walls punctuated by red doors; and an angled glass ceiling encases the cloister-like walkways. Even the staircase railings are well-designed: single steel rods traced along geometric pathways.
