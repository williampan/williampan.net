Started Kurosawa's *Seven Samurai* yesterday and finished it today. Online reviews often remark on how it originated the trope of the "gathering of heroes", or how it compares to *The Magnificent Seven*, or how influential it is in the history of cinema. I have always been uninterested in such discussions. There are things of more substance in the film itself: the flicker of the flowers in the hill, the speed and shimmer of the katana, the black weight of the burial mounds above the three remaining men. The village transforms from a generic collection of houses to a place of deep specificity, as the map and the battle scenes reveal its geographic intricacies. The tense uncertainty of war reflects itself in the spontaneous incursions interleaved within defensive steps planned days earlier. The water wheel spins as another cycle plays out in Kikuchiyo's recognition of himself in the child. The samurai are established from the outset as contrasting characters: some are introduced by their actions (the taking of tonsure, the killing of a thief); some are characterized by words from their colleagues ("a man obsessed only with testing the limits of his skill"); some are left unmemorable to make the others more memorable. The six, meanwhile, stay true to their character to emphasize the dramatic changes in Kikuchiyo's identity. All this is more interesting than Kurosawa's influence on an American cowboy movie.
