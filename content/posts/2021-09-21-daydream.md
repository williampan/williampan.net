In a recording industry dominated by love ballads, few songs talk about finding direction within a chaotic world with crimson skies. But what truly stands out is how it sounds: the deep, continuous guitar line; the occasional rumble of drums; the use of only two similar end rhymes, *ong* and *ou*, that reverberate from start to end (a feature impossible to replicate in English). The music lifts and ebbs, but there is no real emotional climax, no declaration of love or anguish, just steady resolve. 

Laolang and Ding Wei both rose to fame as a campus folk song singers in the 1990s, when the genre took hold in China after increased cultural exchange with Taiwan. Their first major performances were at the same concert in 1994, at the Wutaishan Stadium in Nanjing. This was the first "folk song" movement in China—"folk song" in the American folk revival tradition, with a heavy reliance on acoustic guitar, a genre that remains popular in China today. Laolang would continue recording primarily in a folk style; Ding Wei would venture into jazz and experimental pop. Her first albums—*Butterfly with broken wings* <span lang="zh">斷翅的蝴蝶</span> (1995), *Begin* <span lang="zh">開始</span> (2003), and *Dear Dingwei* <span lang="zh">親愛的丁薇</span> (2004)—still sound more innovative than most Chinese pop produced now, and her album *Untied* <span lang="zh">鬆綁</span>, released in 2017, is even more cohesive and inventive than her early work. Some songs, like "Snow", sound unlike any Chinese music I've heard. 

For many years after her early albums, Ding Wei worked on television and movie soundtracks, often collaborating with Lin Chaoyang, a classical violinist and composer. They worked together on the soundtrack this song is from, for the 2019 television show *Detective Ke Chen* <span lang="zh">神探柯晨</span>, and invited Laolang to record these vocals.
